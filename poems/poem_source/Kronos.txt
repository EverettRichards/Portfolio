3/27/26

There is not much I can say,
no narrative I can conjure,
that the Greeks did not better tell
when they spoke of the Titan King Kronos,

who ruled over time
with hands sharp and true,
and commanded the skies
to tick black and blue.

His children, the gods,
were emblems for man
and his wretched disposition
for hubris and arrogance,

unaware of the spirits
of nature that guide him
through a hostile universe
of uncertain pose.

And Kronos, fearing his demise
at the hands of his young children,
elected to consume them
with jaws mighty and ruthless,

unaware that as he selfishly cannibalized
the heirs to his kingdom,
so too did he inspire
the vengeful hand of Zeus,

who dethroned his father
and wore his pronged crown,
as time's iron fist
subsided to thunder.

And thus, the Greek gods
are no realer than mithril,
for they conquered time
and cast it to Tartarus,

the dungeon abyss,
a prison for Titans,
deep, deep, deep down below,
beneath Hades and Styx.

And we, as mere mortals,
are losing the war
against ages and eons
and time's bitter hand,

a war of attrition
where soldiers march forth
as they spoon-dig beneath them
their moon-shadowed graves.

If only we had
the strength and the valor
to stare down the tyrant
and challenge his throne,

we could write a million novels,
earn a thousand grand trophies,
break bread and eat olives
with our neighbors forever;

we could plant our soft footprints
on every mere inch
of this well-worn-out planet
and then do it again;

we could taste a true freedom
that sinks with the breeze
as it wafts its wispy way
from Olympus to Cairo.

Instead, we abate
in our fight against Kronos
as he chews and digests
our blood, flesh, and humor,

because we are not gods,
nor will we be ever,
and always will we succumb
to the halting hand of time.
