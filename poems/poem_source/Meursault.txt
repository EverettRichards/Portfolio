* This work is inspired by Albert Camus' 1942 novel "The Stranger" (fr. L'Étranger), particularly as translated into English by Matthew Ward.
In the final chapter of the book, the main character, Meursault, awaits execution for his crime of murder.
As he sits in his prison cell, he reflects on his life, and comes to terms with the dissonant feelings that had once weighed him down.
I wrote these words while sitting at night by an unlit fireplace that still smoldered from a recent user,
looking through old photos of happier days, and watching the clouds in the light-polluted sky.

...

For a while, I sat amidst lukewarm coals
and thought of words I'd read last month,
as I watched the under-lit clouds
wander across the city-baked sky,
like orphaned doves on a cold boulevard,
waiting eternally for a doomed reunion,
or like the incessant mellow ramblings
of a tameless but weary toddler,

and I reminded myself of warm years hence,
when heartache felt hopeful in some bizarre sense,
and still I'd pretend that my stagnance had merit,
and convinced myself I could win life's strange race
by merely accepting my virtues within,
without stepping foot outside my room's door,
a dark, keyless cage where I keep my designs
and plans for a future I cannot devise,

and just like Meursault, as he waited for death,
in that night alive with signs and stars,
much like my own night, only darker, and clearer,
I opened myself to the gentle indifference of the world,
and let the brotherly hand of Kronos twist its fingers between mine,
apprehensive -- not afraid, but uncertain --
of what the new day's light might expose.
